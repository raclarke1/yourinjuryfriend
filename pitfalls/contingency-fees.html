Contingency Fees — What You Need to Know

After an injury, finding an ethical and skilled lawyer can feel overwhelming, but understanding how they charge you for representation is one of the most important factors to consider when choosing which attorney to hire.

Most personal injury lawyers work on a "contingency fee" basis, and, rarely, you might encounter a personal injury attorney who charges by the hour.

Contingency Fee Lawyers (Most Common in Injury Cases) With a contingency fee, your lawyer only gets paid if you win. Period. If there's no recovery, you don't owe them anything for their time.

Most Common Fee Structure: - Up to 33% if your case is settled before trial - Up to 40% if your case goes to trial - Up to 45% if your case is appealed

For example, if your case settles for $100,000, your attorney will typically receive between $33,000 and $45,000.

Advantages: - There are little to no up-front cost; - The lawyer shares your risk; - The lawyer is incentivized to produce high quality work; - You gain access to top-quality legal representation, regardless of your financial situation; - Attorneys are more selective about which cases they take.

Disadvantages: - The total fee might be more than if you paid hourly; - You may feel pressured to settle early; - The percentage the attorney keeps is substantial on larger settlements.

Case Costs: The Not-So-Hidden Fees Even with contingency fee arrangements, many attorneys require clients to cover 'case costs': - Filing Fees ($200 – $500) - Medical Record Retrieval ($50 – $500) - Expert Witness Fees ($1,500 – $15,000) - Deposition Costs ($500 – $2,500 per deposition) - Mediation Fees ($1,000 – $3,000) - Trial Exhibits ($500 – $5,000)

Understanding the Contingency Contract Pay special attention to: - Fee Percentages at different stages - How are case costs handled - Whether the attorney's percentage is calculated before or after the costs are deducted - Termination clauses

Hourly Lawyers (Rare for Injury Cases) Hourly rate varies: $300–$1,000 per hour.

Pros: More involvement in strategy, potentially cheaper for simple matters, keep 100% of settlement. Cons: You pay up front, risk paying and still losing, unpredictable costs, need substantial financial resources.

Hybrid Fee Arrangements Some attorneys offer: - Reduced hourly rate plus smaller contingency - Sliding scale contingency - Flat fees for specific phases

Ask These Questions Before You Sign Anything - What percentage will you take if we settle? If we go to trial? If we appeal? - Who pays for case costs? - Are costs deducted before or after your percentage is calculated? - Can you provide an estimate of what my take-home amount might be? - What happens if I terminate our agreement?

Bottom Line Contingency fees are usually the better option unless you're in the rare situation where you're extremely likely to recover a large amount early. Get everything in writing.
